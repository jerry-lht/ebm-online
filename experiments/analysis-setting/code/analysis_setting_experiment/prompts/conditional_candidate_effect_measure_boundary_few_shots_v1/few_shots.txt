Example 1
Outcome evidence mentions depressive symptoms measured with the Beck Depression Inventory in some studies and the Hamilton Depression Rating Scale in others.
Correct label: Std. Mean Difference
Why not Mean Difference: the construct is shared, but the instruments and score ranges differ across studies.

Example 2
Outcome evidence states that change in symptom score from baseline to 12 weeks was compared between arms.
Correct label: Mean Difference Change from Baseline
Why not Mean Difference: the target is the between-arm contrast in change scores, not the endpoint score alone.

Example 3
Outcome evidence reports mean depression score at end of treatment, with no statement that change scores were synthesized.
Correct label: Mean Difference
Why not Mean Difference Change from Baseline: endpoint or post-treatment scores should stay Mean Difference unless the review explicitly synthesizes change from baseline.

Example 4
Outcome evidence reports adjusted odds ratios from logistic regression for treatment response.
Correct label: Odds Ratio
Why not Risk Ratio: the review explicitly frames the synthesis in odds terms and uses logistic-model outputs.

Example 5
Outcome evidence states that rare adverse events were pooled with the Peto odds ratio method.
Correct label: Peto Odds Ratio
Why not Risk Ratio: the review explicitly names the specialized Peto rare-events method.

Example 6
Outcome evidence describes absolute risk reduction in serious adverse events.
Correct label: Risk Difference
Why not Risk Ratio: the target is an absolute difference in risk, not a ratio of risks.

Example 7
Outcome evidence reports the proportion of participants with remission and gives risks in each arm, without odds-based modeling.
Correct label: Risk Ratio
Why not Odds Ratio: ordinary binary event outcomes should default to Risk Ratio unless the review explicitly uses odds or logistic-model outputs.

Example 8
Outcome evidence reports hazard ratios for overall survival.
Correct label: Hazard Ratio
Why not Rate Ratio: survival and time-to-event analyses are hazard-based rather than person-time rate contrasts.
